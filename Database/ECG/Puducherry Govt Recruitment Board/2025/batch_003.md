## Q51
Question: What is a motor point in a muscle?
Option A: The location where sensory nerves exit the muscle
Option B: The point where the muscle attaches to bone
Option C: The site where the motor nerve enters the muscle, making electrical stimulation most effective
Option D: The area of the muscle with the least blood supply
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: The motor point is the specific area on the skin surface corresponding to the site where the motor nerve enters the muscle belly. Because this is where the nerve branches are most concentrated, applying electrical stimulation at this point results in the greatest muscular contraction with the least amount of current.

## Q55
Question: Which of the following statements correctly describes the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)?
Option A: It controls voluntary muscle movement through direct innervation of skeletal muscles
Option B: It consists only of a single neuron pathway from the CNS to the effector organ
Option C: It only regulates brain activity and sensory perception
Option D: It controls involuntary functions and includes preganglionic fibres from the CNS, ganglia and postganglionic fibres to smooth muscles and glands
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: The autonomic nervous system (ANS) regulates involuntary physiological processes such as heart rate, digestion, and glandular secretion. Unlike the somatic system, it utilizes a two-neuron pathway: preganglionic neurons originating in the CNS that synapse in autonomic ganglia, and postganglionic neurons that project to smooth muscles, cardiac muscle, or glands.

## Q61
Question: Muscle tremor artifacts on an ECG can be misinterpreted as which of the following?
Option A: Atrial flutter
Option B: Atrial fibrillation
Option C: Ventricular tachycardia
Option D: All of the above
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Muscle tremors, often caused by shivering, nervousness, or neurological conditions, create high-frequency baseline noise on an ECG. This "artifact" can mimic the rapid, irregular activity seen in atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, or even the wide-complex appearance of ventricular tachycardia, potentially leading to a misdiagnosis.
