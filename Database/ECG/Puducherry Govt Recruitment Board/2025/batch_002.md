## Q26
Question: Which of the following drugs is known to inhibit drug-metabolizing enzymes?
Option A: Allopurinol
Option B: Omeprazole
Option C: Rifampicin
Option D: Both (A) and (B)
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Both allopurinol and omeprazole are known to act as inhibitors of certain cytochrome P450 enzymes. ## Q27
Question: Which of the following drugs has the potential to cause haemolysis in individuals with G-6PD deficiency?
Option A: Paracetamol
Option B: Ibuprofen
Option C: Metformin
Option D: Primaquine
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency leaves red blood cells vulnerable to oxidative stress. Certain drugs, most notably the antimalarial agent primaquine, act as oxidizing agents that can trigger acute haemolysis in individuals lacking this protective enzyme.

## Q28
Question: Which of the following statements about Propantheline is true?
Option A: It is primarily used today as a first-line treatment for peptic ulcers.
Option B: It enhances gastric emptying and has a short duration of action.
Option C: It is an anticholinergic drug that reduces gastric secretion and delays gastric emptying.
Option D: Its clinical use has increased with the development of H2 blockers and proton pump inhibitors.
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Propantheline is a synthetic anticholinergic (antimuscarinic) agent. By blocking muscarinic receptors in the stomach, it decreases gastric acid secretion and slows gastrointestinal motility (delays gastric emptying). Its use has declined significantly following the advent of more effective acid-suppressing drugs like H2-receptor antagonists and PPIs.

## Q31
Question: The return of heart muscle cells to their resting state following stimulation (depolarization) is called
Option A: Conductivity
Option B: Automaticity
Option C: Repolarization
Option D: None of the above
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: After cardiac cells are stimulated and depolarized (contraction phase), they must restore their resting membrane potential. This electrical recovery process is known as repolarization, which allows the cells to become excitable again for the next cardiac cycle.

## Q34
Question: The ________ is a small collection of specialized cells capable of automatically generating an electrical stimulus and functions as the normal pacemaker of the heart.
Option A: Atrioventricular node
Option B: Sinoatrial node
Option C: Bundle of His
Option D: Atrioventricular junction
Correct Answer: Option B

## Q35
Question: ECG waves are labelled alphabetically and begin with the ________ Wave.
Option A: P
Option B: QRS
Option C: U
Option D: ST segment
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Standard ECG nomenclature follows an alphabetical sequence established by Willem Einthoven. The P wave, representing atrial depolarization, is the first distinct wave in the cardiac cycle followed by the QRS complex and the T wave.

## Q36
Question: Which of the following is classified as a shockable rhythm in cardiac arrest?
Option A: Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)
Option B: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)
Option C: Asystole
Option D: Brady-asystolic rhythm
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Ventricular fibrillation (VF) and pulseless ventricular tachycardia are the two primary shockable rhythms in cardiac arrest. Defibrillation is essential for these rhythms, whereas PEA and asystole are non-shockable rhythms that require CPR and epinephrine.

Option A: Brady-asystolic rhythm
Option B: Ventricular tachycardia
Option C: Electromechanical Dissociation (EMD)
Option D: Torsades de pointes
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA) refers to a state where the ECG shows an organized electrical rhythm, but there is no detectable pulse or mechanical cardiac output. This is historically and synonymously referred to as Electromechanical Dissociation (EMD).

## Q38
Question: What is the result of sinus arrest if no subsidiary pacemaker takes over?
Option A: Atrial fibrillation
Option B: Ventricular fibrillation
Option C: Ventricular standstill (asystole)
Option D: Supraventricular tachycardia
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Sinus arrest occurs when the SA node fails to generate an impulse. If secondary or tertiary pacemakers (like the AV node or ventricular escape rhythms) also fail to initiate an impulse, the heart will cease electrical activity, resulting in asystole (ventricular standstill).

Option B: Blood loss from trauma or surgery
Option C: Inadequate dietary intake of vitamin B12 and folic acid
Option D: Excessive destruction of RBCs not compensated by increased production
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Hemolytic anemia occurs when the rate of red blood cell destruction exceeds the bone marrow's ability to produce new cells. While the bone marrow often increases production in response, the anemia manifests when that compensatory production is insufficient to maintain normal hemoglobin levels.

Option A: Iron deficiency
Option B: Excessive blood loss
Option C: Bone marrow suppression due to toxins
Option D: Deficiency of folic acid leading to impaired DNA synthesis
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Megaloblastic anemia is caused by a failure in DNA synthesis, which typically results from a deficiency of vitamin B12 or folic acid. This impairment prevents the maturation of RBC precursors in the bone marrow, leading to the formation of characteristic, abnormally large, immature red blood cells (megaloblasts).

## Q50
Question: Which of the following is not a recognized morphologic type of arteriosclerosis?
Option A: Senile arteriosclerosis
Option B: Hypertensive arteriolosclerosis
Option C: Mönckeberg’s arteriosclerosis
Option D: Thromboangiitis obliterans
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Arteriosclerosis is a general term for the hardening of arteries. Recognized types include atherosclerosis, Mönckeberg’s medial calcific sclerosis, and arteriolosclerosis (associated with hypertension). Thromboangiitis obliterans (Buerger's disease) is a distinct inflammatory vascular disease (vasculitis) that causes thrombosis, not a type of arteriosclerosis.
Source: Original PYQ
