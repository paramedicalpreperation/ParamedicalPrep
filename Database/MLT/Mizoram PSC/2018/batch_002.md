## Q31
Question: Structural formula of Haemoglobin-S is:
Option A: Alpha2 Beta2 6glu—valine
Option B: Alpha2 Beta2 6glu—lysine
Option C: Alpha2 Beta2 121glu—glycine
Option D: None of these
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: In Hemoglobin S, a single point mutation results in the substitution of glutamic acid with valine at the 6th position of the beta-globin chain.
## Q32
Question: Percentage of Normal HbA is about:
Option A: 96%
Option B: 50%
Option C: 80%
Option D: 45%
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: HbA (Adult Hemoglobin) constitutes the majority of hemoglobin in healthy adults, typically ranging from 95% to 98%.
## Q33
Question: Plasma cells are derived from:
Option A: Platelets
Option B: Lymphocytes
Option C: Monocytes
Option D: RBC
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Plasma cells are terminally differentiated B lymphocytes that have been activated to produce and secrete antibodies.
## Q34
Question: Normal life span of Platelets is about:
Option A: 120 – 140 days
Option B: 2 – 3 months
Option C: 7 – 14 days
Option D: 1 – 2 days
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Platelets (thrombocytes) circulate in the blood for an average of 7 to 10 days before being sequestered and destroyed by the spleen.
## Q35
Question: The cells responsible for immunity is:
Option A: Neutrophils
Option B: Lymphocytes
Option C: Monocytes
Option D: Both (a) and (c)
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Lymphocytes (T cells and B cells) are the primary cells of the adaptive immune system, responsible for specific immune responses.
## Q36
Question: In female, due to menstruation, the monthly loss of iron is about:
Option A: 2 – 5 mg
Option B: 15 – 28 mg
Option C: 1.5 – 2.8 mg
Option D: None of these
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Menstrual blood loss in a healthy female typically results in an iron loss of approximately 15 to 28 mg per cycle.
## Q37
Question: High rise of ESR will be found in:
Option A: Multiple myeloma
Option B: Malaria
Option C: Typhoid fever
Option D: None of these
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Multiple myeloma is characterized by the production of high levels of monoclonal proteins (paraproteins), which cause RBCs to aggregate into rouleaux, leading to a significantly elevated ESR.
## Q38
Question: Haemolytic disease of the newborn due to Rhesus incompatibility depends upon the:
Option A: The mother possessing RH antigens not present in the red cell of baby
Option B: Inability of the baby to react against the red cells of mother
Option C: Transplacental passages of IgM anti-Rh antibodies
Option D: Transplacental passages of IgG anti-Rh antibodies
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Hemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN) occurs when maternal IgG anti-Rh antibodies cross the placenta and destroy the fetal red blood cells.
## Q39
Question: Which one of the statement is incorrect? The distribution of macrophages in:
Option A: tissue is as “histiocytes”
Option B: in sinusoid of liver – as “Kupffer’s Cells”
Option C: in bone marrow, Sinusoid of spleen as “Mast Cell”
Option D: in blood as “Basophils”
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: The statement (c) is incorrect because macrophages in the bone marrow and spleen sinusoids are not Mast cells; they are tissue-resident macrophages. Additionally, in blood, monocytes (not basophils) are the precursors to macrophages.
## Q40
Question: Mast cells produce:
Option A: Glycoproteins
Option B: Histamine
Option C: Help formation of hyaluronic acid
Option D: Heparin
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Mast cells are well known for storing and releasing histamine, a key chemical mediator in allergic and inflammatory responses.
## Q41
Question: The following are the characteristics of epithelial tissue, except:
Option A: The constituent cells are closely packed
Option B: It has capillaries
Option C: The cells rest on basement membrane
Option D: The surface cells are destroyed by wear and tear
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Epithelial tissue is avascular; it does not contain its own capillaries and relies on underlying connective tissue for nutrient diffusion.
## Q42
Question: Renal biopsy for paraffin embedding is preferred to be fixed with:
Option A: Clark’s fluid
Option B: Neutral buffered formalin
Option C: 10% formal saline
Option D: All of these
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Neutral buffered formalin is the standard fixative for renal biopsies, as it prevents the acid-induced damage to delicate glomerular structures sometimes seen with other fixatives.
## Q43
Question: For dehydration of delicate tissue it is recommended to start processing with:
Option A: 20% Ethanol
Option B: 40% Ethanol
Option C: 30% Ethanol
Option D: None of these
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: When processing delicate tissues, starting with a lower concentration of ethanol, typically 30%, minimizes tissue shrinkage and prevents damage during the transition from aqueous fixatives.
## Q44
Question: Clearing agents suitable for routine use includes the following, except:
Option A: Chloroform
Option B: Toluene
Option C: Xylene
Option D: Methanol
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Methanol is a dehydrating agent, not a clearing agent. Xylene, toluene, and chloroform are standard clearing agents used in histology.
## Q45
Question: The temperature of water bath in histopathological laboratory used for floating the paraffin sections should be:
Option A: 10°C below the melting point of paraffin
Option B: 5°C below the melting point of paraffin
Option C: 15°C below the melting point of paraffin
Option D: 20°C below the melting point of paraffin
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: A water bath temperature set to about 5°C below the melting point of the paraffin wax allows the section to flatten perfectly without melting it.
## Q46
Question: The most common gases used for frozen sections of tissue are, except:
Option A: Carbon-dioxide gas
Option B: Liquefied Nitrogen
Option C: Aerosol Spray
Option D: Nitrous oxide
Correct Answer: B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: While liquid nitrogen is used in cryostats, for older or specific "gas-based" freezing techniques, carbon dioxide and nitrous oxide were standard. Aerosol sprays also exist, but liquefied nitrogen is a cryogenic fluid, not a gas-based freezing mechanism in the traditional sense of a "gas-jet" freezer.
## Q47
Question: Masson’s trichrome stain is used to demonstrate:
Option A: Collagen
Option B: Muscle
Option C: Elastic tissue
Option D: Both (a) and (b)
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Masson’s trichrome is a classic stain that differentiates collagen (usually blue or green) from muscle fibers and keratin (usually red).
## Q48
Question: Best’s Carmine stain is used to demonstrate, except:
Option A: Lipids
Option B: Glycogen
Option C: Mucin
Option D: Fibrin
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Best’s Carmine is a specialized histochemical stain primarily used to demonstrate glycogen; it does not stain lipids.
## Q49
Question: Hematogenous endogenous pigments include the following, except:
Option A: Bile fragments
Option B: Porphyrins
Option C: Chromaffin
Option D: Haemosiderins
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Chromaffin is an endogenous pigment related to neuroendocrine cells, but it is not derived from the breakdown of blood (hematogenous) like bilirubin (bile), porphyrins, or hemosiderin.
## Q50
Question: Malarial pigment is an:
Option A: Endogenous mineral
Option B: Non-haematogenous endogenous pigments
Option C: Artefact pigments
Option D: Exogenous pigments
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Malarial pigment (hemozoin) is formed from the breakdown of hemoglobin, making it hematogenous, but it is technically considered an endogenous pigment related to the parasite's metabolism.
