## Q1
Question: What is the life span of RBC?
Option A: 120 days
Option B: 100 days
Option C: 200 days
Option D: 80 days
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: The average life span of a normal red blood cell (RBC) in circulation is approximately 120 days, after which it is destroyed by macrophages in the spleen and liver.
## Q2
Question: What is the recommended cleaner for removing all oil from objective lens?
Option A: 70% alcohol or lens cleaner
Option B: Xylene
Option C: Water
Option D: Benzene
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: A mixture of 70% alcohol (like isopropyl or ethyl alcohol) or commercial lens cleaner is recommended for safely cleaning immersion oil off objective lenses without damaging the lens cement. Xylene is toxic and can dissolve the lens cement over time.
## Q3
Question: Serum differs from blood as it lacks
Option A: Antibodies
Option B: Clotting factors
Option C: Albumins
Option D: Globulins
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Serum is the fluid portion of blood obtained after the blood has been allowed to clot. Because the clotting factors (like fibrinogen) are consumed in the clotting process, serum differs from plasma (and whole blood) primarily by lacking these coagulation factors.
## Q4
Question: Oxygen is supplied from lungs to tissues and organs by
Option A: Red blood cells
Option B: White blood cells
Option C: Plasma
Option D: Platelets
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Red blood cells contain hemoglobin, a specialized protein that binds reversibly with oxygen in the lungs and releases it in the peripheral tissues.
## Q5
Question: Blood cells are produced in
Option A: Kidney
Option B: Lungs
Option C: Bone marrow
Option D: Glands
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Hematopoiesis, the production of all types of blood cells (red cells, white cells, and platelets), primarily occurs in the red bone marrow in adults.
## Q6
Question: By volume, the red blood cells constitute about _____% of whole blood.
Option A: 15
Option B: 25
Option C: 35
Option D: 45
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: The hematocrit (packed cell volume) represents the percentage of RBCs in whole blood, which is normally around 45% in a healthy adult.
## Q7
Question: Which constituent of blood fights against infections?
Option A: Red blood cells
Option B: White blood cells
Option C: Plasma
Option D: Platelets
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: White blood cells (leukocytes) are the primary cellular components of the immune system responsible for defending the body against infectious diseases and foreign invaders.
## Q8
Question: The pH value of blood is normally between _____ and _____.
Option A: 6.0, 6.5
Option B: 7.0, 7.35
Option C: 7.35, 7.45
Option D: 7.45, 7.98
Correct Answer: Option C

Explanation: Normal human arterial blood is strictly regulated and maintained at a slightly alkaline pH range of 7.35 to 7.45.
## Q9
Question: An average adult person has about _____ liters of blood by volume.
Option A: 5
Option B: 5.5
Option C: 6
Option D: 6.5
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: An average adult human body contains approximately 5 liters of blood, representing about 7-8% of total body weight.
## Q10
Question: What mordant is used in Gram staining?
Option A: Crystal violet
Option B: Safranin
Option C: Acid-alcohol
Option D: Gram's Iodine
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Gram's iodine acts as a mordant in the Gram stain procedure. It forms a large crystal violet-iodine complex within the bacterial cell wall, which prevents the primary stain from washing out of Gram-positive bacteria during decolorization.
## Q11
Question: Renal Function Test (RFT) includes:
Option A: Electrolytes
Option B: Total protein
Option C: AST
Option D: ALT
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: A standard Renal Function Test (RFT) panel includes BUN, creatinine, urea, and electrolytes (sodium, potassium, chloride). Total protein, AST, and ALT are part of the Liver Function Test (LFT) panel.
## Q12
Question: Microscopic examination of urine is done to find out:
Option A: RBCs
Option B: Pus cells
Option C: Casts
Option D: All of the above
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Urine microscopic examination evaluates the urine sediment for the presence of cells (RBCs, pus/WBCs, epithelial cells), casts, crystals, and microorganisms.
## Q13
Question: Sample used for Platelet counting is:
Option A: Serum
Option B: Plasma
Option C: EDTA blood
Option D: Citrate blood
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Whole blood collected in an EDTA (purple top) tube is the standard sample for platelet counting and most other routine hematology tests because EDTA preserves cellular morphology and prevents clotting.
## Q14
Question: Rh typing is based on the D-antigen present in:
Option A: Platelets
Option B: RBCs
Option C: Lymphocytes
Option D: Eosinophils
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: The Rh blood group system, including the prominent D-antigen, is defined by the presence or absence of specific proteins on the surface membrane of red blood cells.
## Q15
Question: ESR equipment is known as
Option A: Sahli's tube
Option B: Centrifuge
Option C: Vacutainer
Option D: Westergren's tube
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: The Westergren tube is a graduated glass or plastic pipette used specifically for measuring the Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR).
## Q16
Question: The reagent used in the estimation of Glucose is:
Option A: Urease
Option B: Hexokinase
Option C: Jaffe
Option D: None of the above
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Hexokinase is the enzyme used in the reference method for measuring blood glucose, as it is highly specific and accurate compared to older reduction methods.
## Q17
Question: The commonly used fixative for slide preparation is:
Option A: Benedict's reagent
Option B: Distilled water
Option C: Formalin
Option D: None of the above
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Formalin (10% neutral buffered formalin) is the most widely used routine fixative in histopathology to preserve tissue structure and prevent autolysis.
## Q18
Question: Which one of the following is not included in a routine liver function test?
Option A: Bilirubin
Option B: Bile acid
Option C: SGOT (AST)
Option D: Alkaline phosphatase
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: A routine Liver Function Test (LFT) typically includes bilirubin (total and direct), transaminases (SGOT/AST, SGPT/ALT), alkaline phosphatase, and total protein/albumin. Bile acids are specialized tests not typically included in the routine screening panel.
## Q19
Question: CSF samples are collected by:
Option A: Lab Technician
Option B: Staff Nurse
Option C: Physician
Option D: Ward boy
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is collected via a lumbar puncture (spinal tap), which is an invasive clinical procedure that must be performed by a qualified physician.
## Q20
Question: All the following are motile bacteria except:
Option A: Escherichia coli
Option B: Klebsiella
Option C: Vibrio
Option D: Proteus
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Klebsiella species are characteristically non-motile gram-negative bacilli. Escherichia coli, Vibrio, and Proteus all possess flagella and are motile.
## Q21
Question: Confirmatory test for AIDS is:
Option A: ELISA
Option B: CLIA
Option C: Western blot
Option D: Chromatography
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: While ELISA and CLIA are used as initial screening tests for HIV, the Western Blot test has historically been used as the standard confirmatory test to detect specific antibodies to HIV proteins.
## Q22
Question: The basic component of Leishman stain is:
Option A: Eosin
Option B: Methylene blue
Option C: Malachite green
Option D: Safranin
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Leishman stain is a Romanowsky stain consisting of a mixture of an acidic dye (Eosin) and a basic dye (Methylene blue). The basic component that stains the acidic structures of the cell (like the nucleus) is methylene blue.
## Q23
Question: Which of the following is Romanowsky stain?
Option A: Leishman
Option B: Giemsa
Option C: Jenner's
Option D: All the above
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Romanowsky stains are a family of hematology stains composed of eosin and a demethylated methylene blue. Examples include Leishman, Giemsa, Wright's, and Jenner's stains.
## Q24
Question: Which of these is not a lipid?
Option A: Fats
Option B: Oils
Option C: Proteins
Option D: Waxes
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Proteins are macromolecules composed of amino acids, whereas fats, oils, and waxes are all structurally classified as lipids.
## Q25
Question: Which of the following is usually not found in the urine?
Option A: Magnesium
Option B: Urea
Option C: Uric acid
Option D: Glucose
Correct Answer: Option D
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: In a healthy individual, 100% of the glucose filtered by the glomerulus is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule, meaning normal urine contains no detectable glucose.
## Q26
Question: Testing a refrigerated specimen that has not warmed to room temperature will adversely affect:
Option A: Enzymatic reactions
Option B: Dye-binding reactions
Option C: The sodium nitroprusside reaction
Option D: Diazo reactions
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: Enzymatic reactions on reagent strips (such as those for glucose and blood) are highly temperature-dependent. Using cold urine slows down the enzyme kinetics, potentially causing falsely low or negative results.
## Q27
Question: All of the following are important to protect the integrity of reagent strips except:
Option A: Removing the desiccant from the bottle
Option B: Storing in an opaque bottle
Option C: Storing at room temperature
Option D: Resealing the bottle after removing a strip
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: The desiccant must remain in the bottle to absorb moisture and keep the reagent pads dry. Removing it would rapidly degrade the strips. Storing in an opaque bottle, at room temp, and resealing are all proper care steps.
## Q28
Question: A urine specimen with a pH of 9.0:
Option A: Indicates metabolic acidosis
Option B: Should be recollected
Option C: May contain calcium oxalate crystals
Option D: Is seen after drinking cranberry juice
Correct Answer: Option B
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: A urine pH of 9.0 is physiologically impossible for freshly voided urine. It indicates that the specimen has been sitting unpreserved at room temperature for a long time, allowing urea-splitting bacteria to produce ammonia. A fresh specimen should be recollected.
## Q29
Question: The most significant reagent strip test that is associated with a positive ketone result is:
Option A: Glucose
Option B: Protein
Option C: pH
Option D: Specific gravity
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: The presence of both ketones and glucose on a reagent strip is a significant clinical indicator of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), making the glucose test the most clinically correlated with a positive ketone result.
## Q30
Question: A specific gravity of 1.005 would produce the reagent strip color:
Option A: Blue
Option B: Green
Option C: Yellow
Option D: Red
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Hard
Explanation: The specific gravity reagent pad uses a pKa change of a polyelectrolyte in an alkaline medium. The indicator is bromothymol blue. A low specific gravity (1.005) results in less hydrogen ion release, keeping the pH higher and the pad color blue or blue-green.
## Q31
Question: The coagulase test is done to differentiate:
Option A: Staphylococcus aureus from Staphylococcus epidermidis
Option B: Staphylococcus epidermidis from Neisseria meningitidis
Option C: Streptococcus pyogenes from Enterococcus faecalis
Option D: Streptococcus pyogenes from Staphylococcus aureus
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: The coagulase test is the primary biochemical test used to differentiate coagulase-positive Staphylococcus aureus from coagulase-negative Staphylococci (like S. epidermidis).
## Q32
Question: Cardiac markers are:
Option A: CK, CK-MB, Trop-I
Option B: AST, ALT, Protein
Option C: Carbohydrate
Option D: Glucose
Correct Answer: Option A
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Cardiac markers are proteins and enzymes released into the blood when the heart muscle is damaged. Creatine Kinase (CK), its myocardial band isoenzyme (CK-MB), and Troponin I (Trop-I) are specific markers for myocardial infarction.
## Q33
Question: To which of the following would the term "white cell" NOT be applied?
Option A: Erythrocyte
Option B: Leucocyte
Option C: Lymphocyte
Option D: Monocyte
Correct Answer: Option A

## Q34
Question: Which individuals can receive any type of blood and are considered universal recipients?
Option A: A+
Option B: O-
Option C: AB+
Option D: B-
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Easy
Explanation: Individuals with AB+ blood type have both A and B antigens on their red cells and the Rh factor, meaning they lack circulating anti-A, anti-B, and anti-Rh antibodies, allowing them to safely receive any ABO/Rh blood type.
## Q35
Question: What is found in blood serum that is also in blood plasma?
Option A: Blood cells
Option B: Platelets
Option C: Plasma proteins
Option D: Clotting factors
Correct Answer: Option C
Difficulty: Medium
Explanation: Both plasma and serum contain major plasma proteins like albumin and globulins (antibodies). However, serum lacks clotting factors like fibrinogen because they are used up during the coagulation process to form the serum.
